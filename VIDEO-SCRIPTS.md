# crittr.ai — short-form video script pack (Phase 7.6)

Ten 30-second scripts for TikTok / YouTube Shorts / Instagram Reels.
Each one follows the same skeleton so they're cheap to produce: one
person on camera, maybe a hand-held subject, no set.

Production rules:

- 9:16 vertical, 1080x1920 min.
- Hook in the first 1.5 seconds or nobody watches.
- Always end on the same 3-second card: "crittr tells you the
  verdict in 5 seconds. Link in bio."
- Captions burned in (most views are muted).
- No mention of specific brands or dosages on-camera; those live in
  the app.
- Keep tone warm, curious, confident. Not scary. Not jokey.

Across the pack, the recurring narrator voice is: *a friend who
happens to know a lot about animals, talking to you in their kitchen.*

## 1. "My dog ate grapes"

**Hook (0–2s)**: "Your dog just ate a grape. How worried should you be?"

**Body (2–25s)**:
"Answer: very. Grapes and raisins are toxic to dogs at basically any
dose, and nobody really knows why. The response isn't about dose — it's
speed. If they ate one ten minutes ago, you call poison control or you
go. If they ate one last night and seem fine, you still call, because
the damage shows up slow — in the kidneys, in the next twenty-four
hours. This is one of the few things that a 2am text cannot wait on."

**Card (25–30s)**: "crittr tells you the verdict in 5 seconds.
Link in bio."

## 2. "Three things to watch for after a midnight vomit"

**Hook**: "One vomit is almost never a problem. These three things are."

**Body**:
"Number one — blood. Bright red or coffee-ground dark, doesn't matter,
it's a call to the vet in the morning. Number two — three in a row.
Once is a life event. Three times in an hour is a pattern. Number
three — they won't drink water afterwards. Dehydration sneaks up on
small pets fast. If any of those three happen, that's your answer."

**Card**: same.

## 3. "This one question changed my dog's life"

**Hook**: "My vet asked me a question that I wish I'd thought of earlier."

**Body**:
"Is your dog drinking more water than they used to? Sounds like
nothing. It's actually one of the earliest signs of three or four
different things — diabetes, kidney, Cushing's. My dog was drinking
about half again what he used to. Ten dollars' worth of blood work
caught something small before it was something big."

**Card**: same.

## 4. "Mild limp, 48 hours, no swelling — vet or wait?"

**Hook**: "Dog's got a limp. It's been two days. No swelling. Vet or wait?"

**Body**:
"Short answer: book for tomorrow. The rule of thumb a bunch of vets I
know use: if it's been going on longer than twenty-four hours and it's
not improving, it's past the self-healing window. If the limp is worse
in the morning, also concerning. If they won't put any weight on the
paw at all, that's not 'tomorrow,' that's today."

**Card**: same.

## 5. "Your cat is throwing up foam. Is it an emergency?"

**Hook**: "Your cat is throwing up foam. Is it an emergency?"

**Body**:
"Usually? No. Cats with empty stomachs foam all the time. Cats with
hairballs foam all the time. What you're actually watching for:
are they still eating? Are they still interested in you? Is there
blood? Has it been more than twenty-four hours? If the answer to
those is no, no, no, no — you're fine. If any one flips, time to
call."

**Card**: same.

## 6. "Dog stole a grape and you're frozen"

**Hook**: "You turned around for ten seconds. Your dog ate something
from the counter. Now what?"

**Body**:
"Don't try to figure out what they ate by opening their mouth. Figure
out what's missing from the counter. Photograph it — wrapper and all.
That's what the vet wants. Then call. Emetics can be done at home for
some things and absolutely should not be done at home for others —
and that difference is not something you should guess at."

**Card**: same.

## 7. "Puppy won't eat today — how worried?"

**Hook**: "Your puppy hasn't eaten today. How worried should you be?"

**Body**:
"Puppies under three months can crash fast. Under two pounds, even
faster. What you're checking: are their gums pink and wet, or pale
and tacky? Are they still playing? Did they skip water too, or just
food? A puppy who's off food but playing and drinking can usually
wait half a day. A puppy off food and water is a today problem."

**Card**: same.

---

## 8. "The ten-minute rule"

**Hook**: "If your pet does this for more than ten minutes, go."

**Body**:
"Unproductive retching. Meaning they're heaving, and nothing is
coming up. In a deep-chested dog — Great Dane, German Shepherd,
Standard Poodle — this is the early signature of bloat, which kills
in hours. Ten minutes of dry heaves and a distended belly is not a
'try again in the morning' situation. Get in the car."

**Card**: same.

## 9. "What a 'mild' allergy actually looks like"

**Hook**: "Most dog allergies don't look like allergies."

**Body**:
"They look like licking paws. They look like head-shaking. They look
like a dog who's a little more itchy at bedtime. A rash is the
end-stage. By the time you're seeing a rash, the allergy's been
running for weeks. If your dog's been licking the same paw for four
days and you just noticed, that's when it starts."

**Card**: same.

## Production cadence

- Shoot in batches of five. One afternoon with a ring light and a
  tripod covers a full week.
- Post one every Tuesday and Friday for four weeks, then reassess.
- Track: views, completion rate, comments, DMs mentioning the app.
- If one script hits organically, don't reshoot the same topic — use
  the traction to spin a longer YouTube version.

## What we're not doing

- No influencer partnerships until at least one video does 100k
  organically. Paid seeding comes later.
- No trending-sound chasing. Calm authority voice is the brand;
  sound trends undercut it.
- No fear content. "Seven things that will KILL your dog" is a
  genre we are not in.
